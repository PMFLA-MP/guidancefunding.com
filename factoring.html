Invoice Factoring.

Turn your unpaid B2B invoices into cash today. We buy your accounts receivable at a small discount, so you stop waiting 30, 60, or 90 days for customers to pay.

Apply Now Talk to a specialist
What it is

Get paid now, not in 60 days.

Factoring isn't a loan — it's the sale of your accounts receivable. You deliver goods or services and invoice your customer. Instead of waiting for them to pay, you sell that invoice to a factor, who advances you 70–90% immediately and collects from your customer when the invoice comes due.

Because factoring is based on your customer's ability to pay (not yours), it's available to businesses with weak credit, limited operating history, or rapid growth that outpaces their working capital. The factor holds back the remaining 10–30% as a reserve, releases it when the invoice pays, and deducts a small fee.

How it works

Your customer's credit, your cash.

Factoring is underwritten based on who owes you money, not just your own financial position.

70–90% advance

Receive most of your invoice value within 24–72 hours of submission. The rest (minus fees) comes when your customer pays.

Customer credit, not yours

The factor underwrites your customer's ability to pay. Strong customers = approval, even if your own credit is weak.

No long-term commitment

Sell invoices as needed, or every one. Spot factoring, selective factoring, and full-ledger factoring all available.

Not a loan

Factoring is the sale of receivables — it doesn't create debt on your balance sheet or affect your debt-to-equity ratio.

Non-recourse options

Choose non-recourse factoring and the factor absorbs the credit risk if your customer doesn't pay (subject to eligibility).

Scales with sales

As your invoice volume grows, so does your available funding. No reapplying for each increase.

Pricing factors

Your fee is based on.

Factoring fees are surprisingly simple — a percentage of invoice face value per 30 days.

01

Invoice volume

Higher monthly volume = lower per-invoice rate. $500K/month pricing is much better than $50K/month.

02

Customer credit

Invoices to Fortune 500 buyers price better than invoices to small regional customers.

03

Payment terms

Net-30 invoices cost less to factor than net-90. Longer terms = more risk = higher fee.

04

Industry

Staffing, trucking, and manufacturing are low-friction factoring markets. Niche industries may cost more.

05

Concentration

Too much revenue from one customer = higher risk. Diversified receivables price better.

To apply

Required documents.

  • List of customers and typical invoice amounts
  • Most recent A/R aging report
  • Sample customer invoices and purchase orders
  • Recent business tax returns
  • 3 months of business bank statements
  • Customer contracts (for larger recurring relationships)
Before you apply

Ask yourself.

  • Who are your top 5 customers by revenue? Are they large, creditworthy, and reliable payers?
  • What are your typical payment terms (net-30, net-60, net-90)? Longer terms increase factoring cost.
  • Are you comfortable with your factor contacting your customers directly for verification and collection?
Good to know

Things to know about factoring.

Factoring has its own norms — here are things first-time factoring clients often don't expect.

Customer contact

Your factor contacts your buyers

Factors verify invoices directly with your customers and collect payment. This is normal and professional — but something to be prepared for.

Recourse vs non-recourse

Know which you're signing

Recourse factoring means if your customer doesn't pay, you buy the invoice back. Non-recourse is more expensive but the factor absorbs the credit risk (with limits).

Not debt, but watched

Lenders see it on bank statements

While factoring isn't a loan, future lenders will see the arrangement in your statements and ask about it. Not a problem, just be ready to explain.

UCC filing

Factor files a UCC-1 on your A/R

The factor will file a public notice claiming rights to your receivables. This can complicate getting other types of financing while the facility is active.

Ratio caps

Limits per customer

Factors cap exposure to any single customer (typically at 20% of your total receivables). Concentrated revenue needs to be diversified over time.

Spot vs full-ledger

Two models

Spot factoring lets you choose which invoices to sell. Full-ledger means you sell everything. Spot is more flexible; full-ledger is cheaper per invoice.

Frequently asked

Common questions.

How is factoring different from a line of credit? +
A line of credit is debt — you borrow against your limit and owe it back with interest. Factoring is a sale of receivables — you're not borrowing, you're selling an asset. Factoring doesn't create debt on your balance sheet and is often easier to qualify for.
Will my customers know I'm factoring? +
Yes. The factor will send a notice of assignment to your customers instructing them to pay the factor instead of you. Professional factors handle this well, but if you have customers where this could cause friction, discuss it before signing.
Can I factor with bad personal credit? +
Yes. Factoring qualifying is heavily based on your customers' credit, not yours. Businesses with sub-600 FICO scores factor routinely if their customer base is strong.
What happens if a customer doesn't pay? +
Under recourse factoring, you buy the invoice back (or replace it with a different one). Under non-recourse, the factor takes the loss (within policy limits). Most factoring arrangements are recourse by default.
How fast can I start factoring? +
Initial setup takes 3–7 business days (application, customer verification, legal docs). After that, funding against new invoices is 24–72 hours from submission.
