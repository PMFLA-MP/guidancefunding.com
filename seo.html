What it is

Be the result they click.

Search is the single biggest source of high-intent customers for most small businesses. Someone searching "emergency plumber near me" at 10pm isn't browsing — they're buying. If you don't show up on page one, you're invisible to them.

SEO is the long game of showing up for those searches consistently. It's not a one-time trick; it's a system of optimized web pages, local directory listings, fresh content, and positive reviews that compounds over time. Done right, it becomes the most cost-effective customer acquisition channel you have.

Ranking factors

What moves the SEO needle.

Google's ranking algorithm weighs hundreds of factors. These are the ones that matter most for small business.

01

Relevance & intent

Does your page match what the searcher is actually looking for? Intent-mismatched pages never rank, no matter how optimized.

02

Backlinks

Links from other credible sites to yours. Quality matters far more than quantity — one link from a local news site beats 50 from directory spam.

03

Google Business Profile

For local search, your GBP is more important than your website. Complete profile, regular posts, and reviews drive the map pack.

04

Reviews

Review count, recency, and star rating directly influence local rankings. 50 recent reviews beats 200 reviews from 5 years ago.

05

Technical health

Site speed, mobile usability, and clean code aren't ranking boosters so much as ranking gatekeepers. Fail these and nothing else works.

Good to know

Things to understand about SEO.

SEO is simple in concept and hard in execution. Here's what separates effective SEO from expensive theater.

Time horizon

3–6 months minimum

SEO is cumulative. Meaningful traffic growth takes 3–6 months for local businesses, 6–12 months for competitive national categories. Anyone promising faster is either lying or running paid ads.

Local vs national

Different game entirely

Local SEO is about dominating map pack results in your service area. National SEO is about outcompeting the entire internet for organic results. Local is much more winnable for most small businesses.

GBP is the biggest lever

Often overlooked

Your Google Business Profile drives more leads than your website for most local businesses. And it's free. We spend disproportionate effort there first.

Quality > quantity

10 great pages beat 100 weak ones

Google has gotten very good at identifying thin content. Publishing 2 thoughtful, useful articles per month beats publishing 20 mediocre ones.

Reviews compound

Systems beat campaigns

Running occasional "ask for reviews" pushes doesn't work long-term. Systematic review requests after every transaction do. We set up the system.

No black-hat tactics

Short-term wins, long-term losses

Some agencies still use link farms, keyword stuffing, and PBNs. These can work for a few months — then Google catches up and your site tanks. We don't touch any of it.

Frequently asked

Common questions.

How much does SEO cost? +
For small business local SEO, $500–$2,500/month depending on scope, competitiveness, and content volume. National SEO in competitive categories starts around $3,000/month. We'll scope to your goals and budget.
How long until I see results? +
Local SEO typically shows meaningful results in 3–4 months: improved map pack ranking, more GBP views, more calls. Broader organic rankings take 6–12 months. You should see some metric moving in 60 days; if nothing is, something's off.
What's the difference between SEO and paid search (Google Ads)? +
Paid search (Google Ads) puts you at the top immediately, but only while you're paying. SEO takes months to build but keeps producing traffic after the work is done. Most businesses should do both — paid for immediate pipeline, SEO for long-term compounding.
Do you guarantee rankings? +
No legitimate SEO company guarantees specific rankings. Google's algorithm changes constantly and nobody controls the results. What we guarantee is the work: the pages we build, the optimizations we make, the content we publish, the links we earn, and the reporting we deliver.
Will you write the content yourselves, or do I need to? +
We can do either. Some clients prefer to write (they know their industry best); we then edit and optimize. Others want us to handle everything — we have writers in most major verticals. Discuss the tradeoffs and decide together.
